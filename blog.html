Reflection on the reading:

“Convention and Context, Mailoux, 1983” was definitely an interesting and insightful read, it reminds the designers of the importance of considering both established design conventions and the specific context of users when creating digital interfaces. While we as designers should be familiar with the principles and patterns that users have come to expect with websites, they must also be attuned to the specific needs, goals, and audiences of their design projects. By balancing the two, we can create experiences for the user that are intuitive, user-friendly, and tailored to the content presented. Mailoux's emphasis on the interplay between convention and context encourages us to continually reassess and iterate upon our work, ensuring that their designs evolve in response to ever-changing user expectations and technological advancements.

Reflection on the IxD Process Lecture:

Goal Alignment: My goal with the website is to create a modern, minimalist and elegant style that is easy to navigate for the viewers and is easy to read. The users I designed this website for are one for the markers, two for like minded individuals that I may potentially form partnerships for future opportunities as well as for my future employment as a web designer. My content is based on this course’s work as well as to show my decision making and growth as a web designer for my future portfolio.

Information structure: The information structure of my website is clear and simple with minimal amount of distractions for the viewer, all the information is structured to be easily read and understood and clearly marked so if one wishes to seek out a specific section of my work, it should be relatively easy.

User Flow: The user flow of my website is designed to be nonlinear, the user is first presented with the home page that displays a small, but relative information about me and then they are free to explorer the different pages as they see if, the information of each page are displayed linearly, however everything is clearly labelled so that if they wish to seek out a specific piece of information, they could easily find it.

Interface Elements: Due to my website being relatively simple, the only interface elements are the buttons to swap from page to page, I find too many interface elements a page to be cluttered and often causes confusion for the viewer.

Design: My design document is getting filled as I continue with my website. Once the document is finalised I will add it to my design page, however so far I am very much liking the design I have managed to create. The wireframe design also needs to be updated with the changes I’ve made.

Reflection on the reading:

The main thing I got from this week’s reading is that we as designers cannot create a design that would cater to all and we can’t expect our audience to obtain the same experiences with our designs as we did. The authors argue that UX should not solely focus on usability and functionality but also on intangible, experiential aspects that can create positive, memorable, and meaningful interactions between users and technology, thus we as designers have to take a more comprehensive approach to UX designing that encompasses all aspects of user experience, ultimately leading to the creation of more engaging, enjoyable, and meaningful websites.

Reflection on Graham et al. - 2015 - Towards a study of information geographies:

The reading underscores the significance of geographic considerations in web development between the global south and the north. As a South African web developer, it emphasises to me the need to ensure accessibility and usability across diverse locations and considering factors like internet connectivity and cultural nuances. It also shows the importance of promoting greater participation and representation from diverse backgrounds in web development.

Reflection on Ballatore's 2017 paper on "Digital Hegemonies":

Ballatore's 2017 paper on "Digital Hegemonies" provides a critical perspective on the influence of search engine algorithms on the visibility of local content. It shows the importance of understanding and optimising these algorithms to ensure the local content is adequately represented in search results instead of digital hegemonies of certain prioritised regions over others and It also emphasises the need to be aware of the potential biases in these algorithms and to strive for a more inclusive and representative digital landscape. These two papers highlight the uneven distribution of digital resources and power as it prioritises information from certain privileged areas of the globe over others and the importance of advocating for more representation of the diverse internet environment across the globe.

References:

Ballatore, A., Graham, M. and Sen, S., 2017. Digital hegemonies: the localness of search engine results. Annals of the American Association of Geographers, 107(5), pp.1194-1215.

Graham, M., De Sabbata, S. and Zook, M.A., 2015. Towards a study of information geographies:(im) mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information. Geo: Geography and environment, 2(1), pp.88-105.

Close reading Digital inequalities in the age of artificial intelligence and big data:

In Digital inequalities in the age of artificial intelligence and big data, the author Lutz provides a detailed review on the digital inequalities in the modern internet landscape. The article presents three different levels of digital divide being the access to digital technology, digital skills and technology use and the results and outcome from the use of technology.

The first level of digital divide refers to the unequal access of the internet, where Lutz argues that while the general access of the internet is widespread in the rich countries of the world, the access to the specific parts of the internet such as social media, mobile data, and smart devices and AI powered technology is still just limited to a small portion of the overall digital users which creates a divide between the average people and the more privileged elites of society.

The second level of digital divide is the inequalities of digital skill and technology use. Lutz presents the digital divide and inequalities between the skill gap within the digital space that is often dominated by AI and large data based technologies within the digital economy.

The third level of digital divide is a relatively new concept where it refers to the differences gained from internet usage. Lutz argues that in today’s internet landscape, data traces are an important outcome from internet usage which may grant advantages or disadvantages that are unevenly distributed within society.

References:

Christoph Lutz, 2019, Digital inequalities in the age of artificial intelligence and big data., Human Behavior and Emerging Technologies Volume 1 Issue 2 (p141-148) URL: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1002/hbe2.140 [last accessed: 27/04/2023]

Reading Reflection:

Reflecting on this reading, it highlights the issues of algorithmic colonisation being a form of digital imperialism where Western tech monopolies impose their technologies that were developed with Western centric perspectives on African nations. How CSS Relates to HTML and JS:

I’m honestly already amazed with CSS after finding out some of the stuff you can do with CSS alone to make the website design to be more responsive and sleek, but after working JS with CSS I feel like the possibilities to expand onto my web design are endless. So far I have improved the user navigation through my webpage by adding both the nav links as well as making the header slide up and down in accordance with the user’s scroll control to make users able to access the header at any point in the page.

Responsive Design:

The benefits of responsive design is that it makes the user experience with the website much more enjoyable, having feedback for everything they do makes the website feel lively and encourages the user to continue with the website.

The challenges of responsive design is mainly deciding what sort of responsive design should be implemented where and the code to make them. So far what I have implemented for responsive design have been the change of size for the text when hovered over as well as the smooth scroll function. I have also made the header respond to the user’s scroll so when they scroll down, the header slides up and out of the way so it does not abstract any content while when the user scrolls up it reappears so the user can access the page navigation at any time they wish.
